Making learning and work count

Labour market LIVE from Learning and Work Institute
15 November 2017

  • Unemployment is 1,425,000, has fallen by 18,000 from last month’s published figure (quarterly headline decreased by 59,000) and the unemployment rate is 4.3%, no change on last month and down 0.2 percentage points on last quarter.
  • The number of claimant unemployed is 806,100, up 1,100 on last month, and the claimant rate is 2.3%.
  • The number of workless young people (not in employment, full-time education or training) is 981,000, up 23,000 on the quarter, representing 13.8% of the youth population (up 0.4 percentage points).
  • Youth unemployment (including students) is 517,000, down 28,000 on the quarter.
  • There are 1.8 unemployed people per vacancy. Learning and Work Institute estimates this figure may rise next month.
  • The employment rate is 75.0% (down 0.1 percentage points on last month’s published figure and down 0.1 percentage points in the preferred quarterly measure).

Learning and Work Institute comment

The latest labour market figures published on 15 November contain some worrying signs with employment falling, working age inactivity rising, and increases in involuntary temporary and part-time employment.

Duncan Melville, Chief Economist at Learning and Work Institute, commented:

‘Last month I drew attention to the slowing in employment growth. This month the level of employment has actually fallen for the first time in nearly a year. Unemployment has continued to fall but only because of a large rise in economic inactivity amongst people of working age - up by 117,000 in the quarter to July to September 2017. This is the largest rise in inactivity for close to eight years, since November 2009 to January 2010. Inactivity amongst young people not in full-time education stands out as having risen particularly sharply in the quarter. The conclusion is that unemployment is falling not because people are entering work, but rather because they are exiting the labour market.

These signs that the labour market may be breaking bad are supported by the facts that the percentages of people working in temporary jobs because they cannot find a permanent job and working in part-time jobs because they cannot find a full-time job have been rising in recent months. In addition, the benefits statistics show that claimant unemployment is clearly rising. While overall one or two months of data are not a new trend, there are enough worrying signs in today's numbers to mean that we need to keep a watch out for potential trouble ahead, especially in the context of continuing uncertainty surrounding the Brexit process and the likelihood that interest rates are gradually on the up.

The Chancellor has the opportunity in next week's budget to act to ensure that Universal Credit helps rather than hinders those seeking to return to work and more generally to support employment and economic growth in the light of Brexit uncertainty. ’

Paul Bivand, AD Statistics and Analysis at Learning and Work, added:

‘This release shows a rising trend for the claimant count. We also have at the least a bottoming out of a range of unemployment measures. It is really unfortunate that we do not have a consistent benefit series for people becoming unemployed, as this is the earliest indicator of job losses hitting employment. DWP has temporarily removed the new claims for Universal Credit series due to an issue with the data. ’

Employment fell by 14,000 between April to June 2017 and July to September 2017. In the last 12 months employment has grown by 279,000.

Unemployment fell by 59,000 between April to June 2017 and July to September 2017. and the unemployment rate fell 0.1 percentage points to 4.3% in the quarter, the joint lowest level since 1975.

Economic inactivity rose by 117,000 between April to June 2017 and July to September 2017. and the inactivity rate rose 0.3 percentage points to 21.6% in the quarter.

The small rise in the claimant count takes account of normal seasonal effects but adjusted figures are not published for local areas. The actual number of claimants, nationally, fell by 6,300 in the month to October, compared to the adjusted rise of 1,100. Therefore, it should not be surprising that figures for local areas will show falls compared to the national picture.

The proportion of people leaving the claimant count (or the ‘leavers rate’) has fallen. At 14.7%, it is now well below the level in early 2015 of 18.8%. The number of new claims has fallen. Jobseeker’s Allowance off-flow rates for JSA claimants of short durations increased. Off-flow rates remain at historically high levels.

Youth unemployment is showing a quarterly fall. There are still 517,000 unemployed young people, and 340,000 (4.8% of the youth population) who are unemployed and not in full-time education. However, the inactivity rate for those not in full-time education is sharply up, by 1.2 percentage points in the quarter.

The proportion of unemployed young people (not counting students) who are not claiming Jobseeker’s Allowance and therefore are not receiving official help with job search is now 52.0%.

A total of 73,000 were counted as in employment while on ‘government employment and training programmes’, where the Office for National Statistics continues to count Work Programme (etc.) participants as ‘in employment’ by default. This number rose 3,000 this quarter. Self-employment rose 4,000 this quarter. Employee numbers fell 10,000 in the quarter. Involuntary part-time employment rose this quarter by 16,000 to 1.05 million, 12.4% of all part-time workers.The proportion remains nearly double that in 2004.

Chart 1: UK unemployment (ILO)

The latest unemployment figure is 1,425,000. It has fallen by 18,000 from the figure published last month. On the basis of later claimant count figures, Learning and Work Institute estimates that unemployment may rise, although this remains highly uncertain. The unemployment rate did not change since last month at 4.3%.
Chart 2: Percentage unemployed not claiming Jobseeker’s Allowance

The proportion of unemployed people not claiming Jobseeker’s Allowance has fallen to 43.7%; (622,000).
Chart 3: Youth long-term unemployment (six months and over, 18-24)

Youth long-term unemployment (which can include students) has risen by 1,000 from last month’s figure and is now 157,000.

The youth long-term Jobseeker’s Allowance count (but not UC) remains far behind, at 20,600. The count fell by 500 this month. 32.0% of the youth claimant count continue to be JSA claimants.
Chart 4: Adult long-term unemployment (12 months and over, 25+)

Adult long-term unemployment on the survey measure is now 299,000. The Jobseeker’s Allowance measure is 147,400.
Chart 5: Unemployment rates by age

The 18 to 24 year old unemployment rate (including students) is 10.6% of the economically active – excluding one million economically inactive students from the calculation. The rate for those aged 25 to 49 is 3.2%. For those aged 50 and over it is 2.9%. The quarterly change is down 0.2 for 18 to 24 year olds, down 0.2 for 25 to 49 year olds, and no change for the over-50s.
Chart 6: Young people not in employment, full-time education or training

The number of out of work young people who are not in full-time education (981,000) has risen in the past quarter by 23,000 , or 2.4%. entirely among the inactive, with the number of unemployed young people not in full-time education or training falling.
Chart 7: Youth unemployment

The number of unemployed young people has fallen by 7,000 since last month’s figures, to 517,000.

Meanwhile, the number of young Universal Credit or Jobseeker’s Allowance claimants fell last month by 1,312, to 161,095. There are 177,000 unemployed young people who are not in education, and do not claim Jobseeker’s Allowance, 52% of all unemployed young people who are not students.
Chart 8: Jobseeker’s Allowance and Universal Credit claimant count

The Jobseeker’s Allowance and Universal Credit claimant count rose by 1,100 in October, taking the total to 806,100. In September, the number of lone parents claiming Jobseeker’s Allowance was 63,090. 14.7% of JSA claimants and 7.8% of the JSA/UC claimant count. Lone parents with a youngest child aged five or over can only claim Jobseeker’s Allowance, unless they are in a UC Full Service area or have other reasons for claiming benefit.
Chart 9: Jobseeker’s Allowance – new claims and leavers

The number of new Jobseeker’s Allowance claims fell by 1,600 this month, to 75,800. Meanwhile the number of leavers also fell, by 4,100, to 81,000. These figures are affected by the continued roll-out of Universal Credit. Nevertheless, if there was a downturn, there would be a turn upwards in new JSA claims in areas not covered by Universal Credit Full Service.
Chart 10: Jobseeker’s Allowance – claimant count leavers rate – leavers as percentage of ‘could leave’

Learning and Work Institute estimates that the ‘leavers rate’ – people who have left the claimant count as a proportion of those who could leave it – has fallen to 14.7% continuing several months of falls.
Chart 11: Jobseeker’s Allowance – claimants staying through each three-month threshold (seasonally adjusted)

These measures show a deterioration for claimants at all lengths of unemployment.

The proportion staying beyond three months has risen to 49%. Short-term claimants are mainly supported by Jobcentre Plus. Longer-term claimants continue to be within the Work Programme, though referrals stopped in April.
Chart 12: Jobseeker’s Allowance – proportion of starters in month becoming longer-term unemployed

The proportion of starters becoming 12-month claimants is now 11.9%. This is likely to rise over the next few months as the proportion of starters becoming 9-month claimants has risen by 2.5 percentage points over the last three months.

These figures are based on those in Chart 11, but show the patterns of the same people passing through successive quarterly thresholds.
Chart 13: Vacancies – whole economy survey

Vacancies (in the Office for National Statistics survey of the whole economy) fell slightly this month, to 780,000. As the number of vacancies is quite volatile, and frequently revised, the Office for National Statistics uses a three-month average.
Chart 14: Unemployed people per vacancy

There are 1.8 unemployed people per vacancy. Learning and Work Institute estimates this figure may rise slightly next month.
Chart 15: UK employment

Employment fell by 46,000 on the figure published last month, to 32,059,000.
Chart 16: Employment rate in the UK

The employment rate fell by 0.1 percentage points over the quarter, to 75.0%.
Chart 17: Claimants for inactive benefits and the economically inactive – inactivity benefits

The number of people inactive owing to long-term sickness rose, trending up, while benefit figures fell.

This chart shows claimants of Employment and Support Allowance, and Incapacity Benefit (the orange dots), compared with survey figures for the economically inactive owing to long-term sickness.

The Department for Work and Pensions’ figures will include those who are appealing against being found fit for work.
Chart 18: Claimants for inactive benefits and the economically inactive – lone parents

The survey figures (showing those looking after family) fell while benefit measures are flat.

Income Support estimates have decreased, and those for Jobseeker’s Allowance are now falling. The rising numbers of lone parents on Universal Credit (Full Service) are not covered.

This chart shows claimants of Income Support as lone parents, plus lone parents claiming Jobseeker’s Allowance (the orange dots) and survey figures for all those who are economically inactive looking after family (including couple families).
Chart 19: Employment rate quarterly change in regions – July to September 2017

This quarter, 5 regions showed a rise in the employment rate, led by the South East and the East of England. The employment rate fell in 7 regions, led by Northern ireland and the South West.
Chart 20: Unemployment rate quarterly change in regions – July to September 2017

7 regions showed an improvement in the unemployment rate this quarter. 5 showed a worsening. The rises were led by the North West and Yorkshire & the Humber .
Chart 21: Inactivity rate quarterly change in regions – July to September 2017

Overall, there was a 0.3 percentage point rise in the inactivity rate. 10 regions showed rises in inactivity, led by Northern Ireland and the South West.
